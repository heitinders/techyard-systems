Every week someone asks us to build them a chatbot, and every week we say no — and then build them something that, from a distance, looks like a chatbot but isn't. The word is doing real damage to the conversation about autonomous systems, and it's worth pulling apart.

A chatbot, historically, is a conversational interface over a knowledge base. You type, it retrieves, it paraphrases. It is, at its best, a better search box. It doesn't act. It doesn't own an outcome. It is stateless in the ways that matter.

## Agents have jobs. Chatbots have answers.

An agent — the thing we actually build — has a job. It owns an outcome. "Resolve this ticket" is a job. "Qualify this lead" is a job. "Keep this shipment moving" is a job. The agent might have a conversational interface at one of its edges, but the interface is incidental to the work.

> The agent might have a conversational interface, but the interface is incidental to the work.

This matters to buyers because they're pricing the wrong thing. A chatbot is valued per-query. An agent is valued per-outcome. If you walk into a scoping conversation talking about chatbots, the buyer will price the engagement like it's a knowledge-base integration, and they will be surprised — either at the number you give them, or at the result they get.

## What we say instead

We say "autonomous system" when we're writing, and "agent" when we're talking. The former signals the boundary of the thing (it runs without per-event supervision). The latter signals the shape of the engagement (it has a job, it owns an outcome). Neither invites the per-query pricing model that "chatbot" does.

The word matters. Pick carefully.
