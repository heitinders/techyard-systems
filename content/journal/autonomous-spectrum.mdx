Buyers often ask "is the agent autonomous?" as if it were a binary. It isn't. Autonomy is a spectrum, and the level you pick has enormous cost and risk implications.

## Five levels

**Level 1 — Suggestion.** The agent proposes. A human acts. Minimum risk, minimum leverage. Useful for unfamiliar workflows where trust is still being established.

**Level 2 — Supervised action.** The agent acts on routine cases. A human approves edge cases. This is where most of our engagements live.

**Level 3 — Bounded autonomy.** The agent acts on a defined slice of the workflow without review. A human reviews escalations only. Good for well-understood workflows with clear escalation criteria.

**Level 4 — Broad autonomy.** The agent acts across most of a workflow. Human review is audit-after-the-fact. Requires mature monitoring and high confidence in the agent's calibration.

**Level 5 — Full autonomy.** The agent owns the workflow end-to-end. Human review is aggregate-only. We don't usually sell level 5 and we'd push back hard on anyone who asks for it in a first engagement.

## Why most engagements should stop at level three

The ROI curve is not linear in autonomy level. Moving from 1 to 2 roughly doubles leverage. Moving from 2 to 3 roughly doubles leverage again. Moving from 3 to 4 roughly adds 30%. Moving from 4 to 5 is usually negative — the marginal leverage is eaten by the marginal risk.

Most of our level 3 clients are not interested in moving to level 4. The escalation-review model is fine. The work is getting done. The team trusts the agent. Further autonomy would not change the daily experience meaningfully.

Pick the level that fits the workflow. Resist the temptation to treat higher numbers as better.
