Most of our client engagements are structured around a ten-week pilot. Not eight, not twelve — ten. The number is not sacred, but it's landed there after roughly three dozen engagements, and it's worth explaining why.

## What ten weeks buys you

Weeks 1–2 are discovery. Walking the actual workflow, not the documented one. Identifying decision points. Writing a scope that management and the front-line team both recognize.

Weeks 3–7 are the build. Agent design, data preparation, integration plumbing, early validation against historical data.

Weeks 8–10 are a bounded live pilot. Real data, real traffic, but a bounded slice. Enough to produce measurable evidence of the claimed outcome; bounded enough that if something goes wrong, nothing important breaks.

## What doesn't fit in ten weeks

- Any engagement where the client hasn't agreed on a success metric.
- Any engagement that requires a new integration with a system the client's own engineering team doesn't already run.
- Any engagement that implicitly requires the client to reorganize their operations. (Implicit is the word — explicit org changes are fine.)

When any of those three appear, we scope a two-week discovery engagement first and renegotiate.

## What we've stopped trying to do

We used to try to fit two-week pilots. They don't work — there isn't enough time to measure outcomes. We used to try to fit six-month builds. They don't work either — the scope drifts and the team stops believing in the measurement.

Ten weeks is narrow enough to stay honest and wide enough to prove something.
